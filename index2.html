Is a Decolonialized Roman Catholic Homily in the U.S. Possible?: Beyond Postcolonial Preaching

Edward Foley

DOI: https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.15754560

MST Review 27, no. 1 (2025): 25-57.

Received, 07 Dec; Accepted, 19 May 2025; Published, 15 June 2025

Abstract

This paper juxtaposes decolonial thinking with contemporary Roman Catholic homilizing. The fundamental question is whether a decolonialized Roman Catholic homily is possible in the U.S. After defining postcolonial practice and theory and illustrating its application to preaching, the author explores the nature of decoloniality and Roman Catholic homilizing. Four homiletic lenses and strategies are proposed to contribute to a more decolonialized path. However, given Roman Catholic canon law restrictions, authentic decolonialized preaching in this context is virtually impossible.

Keywords: Decolonialized Preaching • Postcolonial Theory • Homiletics

Introduction

Postcolonial theory emerged in the late 20th century, first through literary fiction like Chinua Achebe's Things Fall Apart. Theorists like Frantz Fanon, Edward Said, Gayatri Spivak, and Homi Bhabha developed this framework. Its intersection with theology began through biblical criticism, offering a "political hermeneutical avenue." Postcolonial preaching theory recognizes preaching as a theological act, integral to worship, and related to pastoral practices.

Postcolonial Preaching and Its Limits

Christian preaching originated as an event before evolving into formal theologies. Postcolonial theory serves as a dialogue partner with contemporary homiletics, helping deconstruct modern "empires" and introducing ambivalence when pondering divine mystery. However, postcolonial theory faces limitations: its conceptual vocabulary is complex and debated, making it challenging for busy preachers. Susan Abraham notes its opacity and lack of unified structure, while Sarah Travis acknowledges its multidimensionality often results in incomprehensible prose.

An Autobiographical Excursus

As a Caucasian male cleric educated in the Midwest, the author's first assignment at a women's college began his transcultural education. Studies in Europe and subsequent work at Catholic Theological Union exposed him to diverse global perspectives. Colleagues introduced him to contextual theology, feminist exegesis, and decolonial thinking. This journey led to examining worship and preaching through decolonial optics to respect marginalized voices.

Decoloniality: A New Homiletic Ripple

Decoloniality differs from postcolonial theory by emphasizing epistemic disobedience and cognitive delinking from Eurocentric thought. Walter Mignolo distinguishes it as "a way, option, standpoint" rooted in Latin American critiques of Eurocentrism. Aníbal Quijano frames it as an "epistemological reconstitution" against coloniality of power. Unlike postcolonial theory's focus on political control, decoloniality prioritizes "border epistemologies" that honor subaltern knowledge.

Roman Catholic Homilizing Within a Benevolent Empire

Roman Catholic homilizing operates within a hierarchical structure resembling an empire. Key characteristics include: 1) Being a liturgical event inseparable from sacramental services 2) Performed by Christ and assembly together 3) Reserved for ordained clergy 4) Rooted in lectionary texts 5) A rhetorical event rather than a text. Canonical restrictions exclude laity and women, making decolonial preaching particularly challenging given demographic disparities (e.g., Latinos constitute 40% of U.S. Catholics but only 8% of clergy).

Embracing the Kin-dom with Holy Subversion

The author proposes four strategies for decolonial homilizing: 1) Positive Theological Anthropology - Embracing humanity's graced nature across racial/ethnic divides 2) New Sources of Wisdom - Honoring non-biblical wisdom traditions and marginalized cultural expressions 3) Rethinking the "Oops" - Using ambiguity to disrupt settled thinking (inspired by Eugene Lowry) 4) Simulating Subaltern Voices - Employing narrative to build empathy through individuation.

Summary

While authentic decolonial homilizing is virtually impossible under current Roman Catholic polity due to canonical restrictions and demographic imbalances, it remains a worthy goal. Achieving this requires clerics to engage in serious self-reflection about their thinking patterns, sources of wisdom, and willingness to be subversively faithful. Though this may trigger conflict, it's necessary for preaching that feeds all people with renewed dignity.
