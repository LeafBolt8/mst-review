Scriptural Echoes and Allusions in the 1726 Constitutions and Rules of the Beatas de la Compañía de Jesus

Maria Anicia B. Co

DOI: https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.15754657

MST Review 27, no. 1 (2025): 58-103.

Received, 19 Dec 2024; Accepted, 03 May 2025; Published, 15 June 2025

Abstract

The 1726 Constitutions and Rules of the Beatas de la Compañía de Jesus contain words and phrases that echo or allude to the Scriptures, particularly the New Testament. This paper demonstrates that although the beatas had no direct access to the Bible, biblical allusions entered their foundational document through liturgy, homilies, prayers, sacraments, and especially the Spiritual Exercises of St. Ignatius. These scriptural echoes attest that their way of life was biblically influenced despite colonial restrictions on vernacular Bible access. The study employs intertextual analysis to identify biblical resonances in the Rules, showing how the Word of God permeated their spirituality through indirect means.

Keywords: Religious of the Virgin Mary • Beatas de la Compañía de Jesus • Mother Ignacia del Espiritu Santo • Scriptural Echoes and Allusions • Indirect Access to Scripture • Consecrated Life for Women

Introduction

The Religious of the Virgin Mary (RVM), founded in 1684 by Mother Ignacia del Espiritu Santo (1663-1748), is an apostolic institute of pontifical right. The 1726 Constitutions and Rules submitted to the Archdiocese of Manila represent the earliest extant document of this congregation. As the 300th anniversary of these Rules approaches in 2026, this paper examines their scriptural foundations, demonstrating how biblical language permeated the document despite the beatas' lack of direct Bible access.

The Historical Context of the 1726 Rules

The Beginning of Christianity in the Philippines

Christianization began formally with Miguel Lopez de Legazpi in 1565. Missionaries from various orders (Augustinians 1565, Franciscans 1578, Jesuits 1581, Dominicans 1587, Augustinian Recollects 1606) evangelized through catechism, sacraments, and church-building. The Doctrina Christiana (1593) contained prayers and doctrines memorized by natives, while liturgical practices and Pasyon performances conveyed biblical stories.

Scriptures in the Evangelization of the Philippines

Despite colonial restrictions on vernacular Bibles (influenced by post-Tridentine Spanish policies), biblical messages reached Filipinos through Mass sermons, catechism, and devotional practices. The Jesuits, spiritual guides to Mother Ignacia's community, regularly engaged Scripture in formation. While direct Bible reading was not promoted, missionaries fostered vernacular literacy, with some translating biblical portions. Vernacular Scriptures circulated more widely only in the late 19th century.

The Beginnings and Growth of the Beaterio de la Compañía de Jesús

Mother Ignacia, a Chinese-Filipina mestiza, founded the beaterio in 1684 after making the Spiritual Exercises under Jesuit guidance. The community grew despite challenges, drafting rules that evolved into the 1726 Constitutions. These Rules survived royal interventions (1755 decree limiting religious status), Jesuit expulsion (1768), and revolution, guiding the community's transition to formal congregation status (1902).

Scriptural Echoes and Allusions in the 1726 Rules

This study employs intertextual analysis (source- and text-oriented) to identify biblical resonances in Chapters 1-2 of the Rules, which focus on spiritual and communal life. Using Beetham's definitions:

  • Allusion: Intentional reference requiring recognition of source
  • Echo: Subtler reference, possibly unconscious

32 provisions show scriptural echoes, primarily from Pauline epistles, Gospels, and Psalms. Examples include:

  • Rule I.8: "by charity Jesus recognizes the sheep of his flock" (John 10:14)
  • Rule II.6: "peacemakers are called children of God" (Matthew 5:9)

Analysis of Selected Provisions

Close reading of key rules reveals biblical influences:

  1. Rule I.2 (Purity of heart): Echoes Matthew 5:8 but shifts from "pure heart" to "interior purity"
  2. Rule I.6 (Humility): Alludes to Philippians 2:3 ("vainglory") and Hebrews 12:2 ("fix eyes on Jesus")
  3. Rule I.8 (Communal love): Direct allusion to John 10:14 ("sheep of his flock")
  4. Rule I.22 (Divine providence): Resonates with Matthew 6:25-34 and Ephesians 2:5 ("rich in mercy")
  5. Rule II.6 (Peacemaking): Clear allusion to Matthew 5:9

Synthesis and Conclusion

The biblical language in the 1726 Rules entered through:

  • Jesuit spiritual direction (especially Ignatian Exercises)
  • Liturgical participation (Mass, sacraments)
  • Devotional practices and sermons

This demonstrates that despite colonial restrictions on vernacular Bibles, the beatas were not deprived of Scripture's influence. Their Rules reflect a relational spirituality centered on:

  • Interior transformation (purity of heart)
  • Christ-like humility
  • Communal unity
  • Trust in divine providence

The study provides evidence for Scripture's indirect transmission in colonial evangelization and offers a methodology for uncovering biblical roots in foundational religious documents.

About the Author

Dr. Ma. Anicia B. Co, RVM, is a member of the Congregation of the Religious of the Virgin Mary. She holds an STL, SThD, and PhD from Catholic University of Leuven.
